manito park website
  • project objective

    Expand on the Manito Park visual identity that I created with a website, solving the current problem of Manito Park’s web resources being scattered, outdated, and inconsistent.

    target audience

    Young adults, students, photographers, artists ,and people who are new to the Spokane area. Expanded more broadly, all potential visitors of Manito Park. Manito stakeholders.

    audience needs

    A usable site that communicates relevant information and history to its visitors so that they may be inclined to visit or support Manito Park.

    deliverables

    A website with subsections for each individual garden of the park.

    my role

    Solo project, UX and web design focus.

  • insights

    I felt the need to develop a website because the online resources for Manito Park were lacking cohesion and do not provide a strong experience to those exploring them. SpokaneCity.org has a set of pages detailing Manito Park as well the gardens within it, but they are not directly connected through links which makes exploring them inconvenient or confusing. To add to this, the other online resource, ManitoPark.org, is very oudated. I set out to create a website that would convey the same type of information that those sites do: history and information about the park.

    I wanted to do a better job detailing the historical significance of the park because it was not thoroughly explored through official online channels previously. I developed a lot of my design decisions around the other, similar websites I researched based on what I thought they were lacking and what I thought was successful as well as through interviews and persona development.

manito park website

After I completed the Manito Park destination poster and visual identity, I set out to create a website based on those resources. You can view more on those two things here. Currently, Manito Park is lacking an effective web presence. What does exist is within the spokanecity.org domain, where information on the park across is spread across multiple pages that aren’t directly linked together. This creates a poor user experience due to the scattered navigation. ManitoPark.org, which was originally the primary web resource for the park, is very outdated but is more in line with a traditional website, which is what I believe Manito Park needs. All the important information about Manito Park should be centralized in one web resource. With this in mind, I asked myself the question: “what do visitors to the site need and what are they looking for?”

Example of SWOT Analysis, Yellowstone Park

To answer this, I looked back on my research (gathered during the development of the visual identity) and researched the websites of other large or important parks. Specifically, I performed an individual SWOT analysis on each of the following websites: Golden Gate Park, Yellowstone Park, and New York’s Central Park. This helped me get an idea of what similar websites were doing well and what they were doing poorly. Additionally, I looked at the current web resources for Manito in order to figure out what content should be on my web pages. I then compiled the necessary material, wrote new content, and based my sketches and prototypes around it so that I wouldn’t be designing around “lorem ipsum” placeholder text.

Landing Page Content Wireframe

Before I began sketching, I created a basic content-focused wireframe in order to figure out what content should be on each page and what its priority was. This is type of data table that shows the priority of the information on the page and how it will be organized. This allows me to lay out the content of the website without regard for its aesthetics. This is a function over form approach to designing a website. I created a unique wireframe for each template that would be on the website. I then began sketching around the content order presented in these wireframes, focused more on the visual aspect of the site and its overall presentation. I used a color-coded sketching style in order to help me see how my interactive and visual elements were distributed across the page.

Blue = Notes
Green = SVGs and purely visual elements
Red = Action Points (such as buttons)
Black = Mark

Sketchbook Excerpts

I sketched out numerous ideas and layouts for each template and imagined how I could implement them. I didn’t want to sketch something that would be too complex for the scale of the project. After creating a large series of sketches, I moved back to the digital realm and started creating mockups of my layouts in Adobe XD. This was to help me get a better idea of how the site’s interactive elements work without spending the time necessary to code a layout that might need massive adjustments down the line. This turned out to be a great thing because I eventually found myself unhappy with the layout that I had established in the prototype and went back to sketching on paper. This time, I tried to push my creativity a lot more and come up with some challenging or unique layouts in order to give myself a set of complex ideas to draw from. Ideas can always be simplified or stripped back later. After this phase of sketching, I was ready to move on to the build phase because I was happy with my interactive elements.

Prototype Screenshot

For this project I used the CSS framework UI Kit because it was a small enough scale project that I felt that I could do most of the styling and coding necessary myself. I then started developing each “organism” that would be implemented on the landing page: the navigation menu, the header, the map of the park, the grid that would be used to present information and imagery, the links and buttons, and finally the page footer. The overall layout is traditional for ease of use. I then began to develop a basic style sheet (using Sass) that would establish my primary colors, typefaces, font sizes, etc. I referred to my identity standards guide for these decisions. I then stitched together each individual piece to create the landing page.

MyMaps Element Screenshot

I used the Google MyMaps service to create the interactive map because it is responsive and scalable. Visitors to the website are also able to save the map itself to their Google account, which gives it more viewing flexibility than a static image. Providing visitors with a digital park map is preferable because it reduces overhead for the park administration and prevents waste (compared to a printed map). The current Manito Park webpage implements a MyMaps element as well, but for mine I implemented my logomarks for the location icons, adjusted some of the content, and added images.

MyMaps Creation Screenshot

Each subsection of the website is dedicated to a specific garden within the park and contains relevant information and imagery. This was a great opportunity to utilize the logos I created for each section of the park and expand on the written content that exists on other websites. I incorporated much more information on the history of the park than currently exists on many official channels. I had already dictated the organization of the content on these pages in my content-wireframes and sketched them, so they didn’t take long to develop.

After creating the first full build of the website I sought out testing and feedback from both potential users as well as fellow designers. I carefully recorded their feedback as well as oddities in how the webpages displayed on different devices and browsers. With this feedback I then went back and worked on refining the content and general usability of the website with more knowledge of what my testers liked. Unsurprisingly, I found that none of my testers interacted with the content carousels that I had placed on the landing page and in some of the subpages, so I removed them and found better methods of displaying their content or removed it entirely.

After I had the website layout complete, I added the finishing touch to the project by going out and taking my own photographs of the park for the website. I would have done this at the beginning of the project, but I started working on the site in the winter before any flowers were blooming. That isn’t the most ideal time to photograph the graph.

Photography Contact Sheet

Overall, I believe that the website solves many of the issues present in the old park website and serves the purpose of keeping all the park’s information in one place. If I were to add on to the project, I would incorporate the content and information from the Friends of Manito website into the main domain because of how important they are to the existence of the park itself. This would also help raise awareness of their contributions to the community Sadly, I wasn’t even aware that Friends of Manito had a website until I had completed this project. This website challenged me and helped me develop my web design skills, but more importantly it helped me test and refine my user experience design process. This aspect of the Manito Park project was developed on my own accord under my own direction, so it was important for me to develop and stick to a process in order to produce good results.
