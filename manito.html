manito park visual identity revamp
  • project objective

    Overhaul Manito Park’s visual identity and update exisitng resouces such as posters and their website.

    target audience

    The primary target audience of this overhaul was young adults, students, photographers, artists ,and people who are new to the Spokane area.

    audience needs

    Appealing promotional material, strong color palette, an accessible website, and history and information that engages them so that they eventually visit the park.

    deliverables

    Logomarks and identity standards guide, a destination poster for the park, and a new website.

  • insights

    Manito Park’s current visual identity is unclear and lacks direction. This new identity is intended to create a more artistic and stylish feeling through the use of organic lines, hand drawn art, and vibrant colors. This needed to be done while also communicating the historical importance of the park to the Spokane area. The printed materials were created with the budget of Spokane Parks and Recreation, as a result all of the materials can be printed on tabloid size (11” x 17”) paper or smaller. The illustrations were intended to appeal to a young adult audience new to the area becuase I found that those who grew up and around Spokane already had some kind of connection with the park. The logomarks for the park were created to be stylistically consistent with each other and colorful. The main logo was designed with six “petals,” each one representing one of the main sections. Each of these sections (called subsets) has a its own logomark, each of which was designed to reflect the flora and fauna of the area it represents in some way.

    The destination poster went through two major iterations; the first was a completely hand drawn with complementary color scheme of red and green. I eventually decided against this and decided to go with a more diverse color palette because I felt it would better represent the park as a whole. The original composition also isolated each section of the park into its own “comic book frame,” and after wandering the park again in my preparation to re-visit the project I decided that creating a more seamless composition would be more appropriate for the park because that is how it is experiences.
